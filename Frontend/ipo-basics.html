Understanding Initial Public Offerings (IPOs)

What is an IPO?

An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the process through which a private company becomes publicly traded by offering its shares to the public for the first time.

Why Do Companies Go Public?

  • To raise capital for expansion.
  • To provide liquidity to early investors and employees.
  • To enhance the company's public profile.

Steps Involved in an IPO

  1. Appointing Advisors: Companies hire investment banks to underwrite the IPO.
  2. Regulatory Filings: The company submits a draft red herring prospectus (DRHP) to the regulatory authority.
  3. Roadshows: The company presents its business model and financials to potential investors.
  4. Price Band and Bidding: The price range is set, and investors place their bids.
  5. Allotment and Listing: Shares are allocated to successful bidders, and the company gets listed on the stock exchange.

Types of IPOs

  • Fixed Price IPO: The company sets a fixed price at which its shares are offered to investors.
  • Book Building IPO: The company sets a price range, and investors bid for shares within this range.

Risks Involved in IPO Investments

Investing in IPOs comes with its share of risks, such as:

  • Market volatility affecting stock prices post-listing.
  • Limited financial history to evaluate the company.
  • Potential overvaluation driven by hype.
